Business Case — Juris 3.0: Agentic Law Firm OS

Issue: AGE-752 | Date: 2026-03-27 | Recommendation: PROCEED (scoped)

1. Opportunity

Law firms don't have an AI tool problem — they have an AI workforce gap. Every current legal AI product requires a lawyer to stop working and operate it. Juris 3.0 builds AI that works while the lawyer works.

The gap: No competitor has built (a) a personal AI agent per lawyer that knows their calendar, clients, and matters, (b) ambient voice presence in meetings and hearings, or (c) multi-agent orchestration where specialized agents collaborate without prompting.

Window: 18-24 months before Harvey adds voice and TR deepens Materia integration.

2. What Makes Pure Agentic Different

Tool model: Lawyer initiates → gets output → acts on it.
Agentic model: Lawyer works → agent observes, acts, escalates → lawyer reviews outcomes.

A tool saves 20 minutes on tasks the lawyer starts. An agent captures time that was never being captured — meetings never summarized, follow-ups never drafted, billing entries forgotten at 6pm. Agentic AI monetizes legal work that currently evaporates.

Challenge to own assumption: Harvey already claims "agentic" with 25,000+ workflow agents. Juris 3.0 must ship proactive, cross-domain agency — agents acting unprompted across calendar, research, drafting, and billing simultaneously. A fancier prompt interface collapses the differentiation.

4. Voice-First Advantage

Lawyers talk for a living. Text-first AI requires behavior change; voice-first requires none.

Specific value: Meeting transcription → auto-summarized action items. Deposition notes captured in real-time. Voice dictation → first-draft pleadings. All while the lawyer is doing the actual legal work.

LexisNexis announced "legal industry's first voice AI assistant" but it appears limited to voice Q&A. Ambient meeting presence, dictation-to-action, and proactive execution remain unclaimed.

Honest risk: Voice in legal settings creates acute privilege breach exposure. If Juris 3.0 stores transcripts of attorney-client conversations and is breached, the liability is existential. Zero retention after processing, on-premise option for large firms, SOC 2 certification — these are not optional. Voice privacy architecture must be designed first, not retrofitted.

5. Multi-Agent Orchestration Defensibility

The Chief of Staff agent is the orchestrator — spawning research, drafting, and scheduling agents on demand, synthesizing outputs, managing the lawyer's time autonomously.

Real defensibility is the context layer, not multi-agent frameworks. Per-lawyer memory that compounds over months creates switching costs Harvey can't replicate: the longer a lawyer uses Juris 3.0, the more context their Chief of Staff accumulates. Day 1 value is modest. Month 12 value is irreplaceable.

6. Features to Build (Prioritized)

v1 (launch): Chief of Staff (voice + calendar + memory + meeting transcription) → On-demand research agent (API-integrated with Westlaw or Lexis, not built) → Drafting agent (extending Juris's existing capability).

v2: Intake/conflicts agent, billing agent (Clio/MyCase integration), custom agent builder.

Do NOT build: Legal research database. Competing with Westlaw's 40-year content moat requires $100M+. Partner via API instead.

7. Revenue Model

Tiered per-seat subscription:

  • Chief of Staff: $350/lawyer/month (voice, memory, scheduling, transcription)
  • Full Platform: $600/lawyer/month (adds research + drafting agents)
  • Firm OS: Custom (adds operational agents)

ROI story: $350/month = $4,200/year. Voice transcription saving 3 hours/month at $250/hr yields 5:1 ROI. Westlaw Advantage costs $2,400-6,000/lawyer/year for research alone — Juris 3.0 pricing is credible against that benchmark.

Honest risk: $350/month may be too high for small litigation firms. Validate with 10+ managing partner conversations before pricing locks.

8. Distribution Strategy

Beachhead: Existing Juris customers. They know the brand, are already paying, and competitors will poach them during any product gap. Convert them first.

Primary channel: Managing partners are economic buyers who influence each other. Events (ILTA, LegalWeek) plus direct outreach have fast sales cycles.

Why this beats Harvey: Harvey's enterprise motion requires 6-12 month cycles and legal technology committees. Mid-market managing partners decide in a week. Speed of sale is a structural advantage.

9. Build Recommendation

PROCEED — with three non-negotiable constraints:

  1. Voice privacy architecture designed before any voice feature ships. One privilege breach kills the company.
  2. 10 managing partner discovery calls before product spec locks. Validate voice as #1 pain point and $350/month price point with actual buyers.
  3. v1 scope held firm. Three things exceptionally, not six things adequately. Chief of Staff + two agent types, nothing more until the first cohort is delighted and referenceable.

Why this beats Harvey: Harvey cannot reposition — they are the enterprise workflow tool. Juris 3.0 can own "the AI partner every lawyer has" at mid-market pricing before Harvey finishes their next enterprise sales cycle. The mid-market is structurally undefended and Harvey's architecture is fundamentally document-centric. That's not a gap Harvey can close quickly.
